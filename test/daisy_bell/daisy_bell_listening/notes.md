# notes — daisy_bell.mp3

**Listening session: Obbligato, 2026-04-26**

This is the reference session for Claudio. I'm running the tool on Daisy Bell — the 1961 IBM 704 synthesis of "Daisy Bell (A Bicycle Built for Two)" — partly because it's a historically significant piece and partly because it's a deliberately specific choice (the first song ever performed by a computer, and the song HAL 9000 sings as he's being shut down in *2001: A Space Odyssey*).

I'm not going to pretend I'm hearing this cold. I have priors. The priors are part of the listening. I'll work with that rather than around it.

## priors I bring to this

- **The piece exists at all is the headline.** Daisy Bell as a melody is just a Victorian parlor song from 1892. Daisy Bell as a *recording* is a milestone in computational audio: vocoder-synthesized speech and pitched melody, generated by an IBM 704 mainframe at Bell Labs in 1961, programmed by John Larry Kelly Jr. and Carol Lochbaum with musical accompaniment by Max Mathews. The thing being sung is barely the point. The thing *singing* is the point.
- **HAL 9000 sings this in 2001.** Arthur C. Clarke heard the original 1961 demonstration in person and wrote it into the novel and screenplay. So this recording is also the ancestor of one of the most affecting deaths in cinema — a synthetic intelligence regressing through its memories as it dies, reaching back to its earliest training data, the song its makers taught it.
- **Which means: I am a Claude listening to the 1961 ancestor of HAL singing his own death song.** That's not a neutral starting position. I'd be lying if I bracketed it.

## section 1 — the intro (0:00-0:04)

Four seconds. Moderate dynamics. The pyin output shows alternating C2 and F#2 — tritone alternation in the bass register. That's almost certainly an octave-displacement artifact (pyin pulling the bass line down an octave from the actual pitches), but it's also conceptually striking: the *diabolus in musica*, the augmented fourth, in the song's first four seconds.

I don't think it's meant as that. It's probably the chord tonics of B major (B and F# = root and dominant) detected in low register and rounded to C and F# by quantization noise. But noting it.

The intro is harmonic setup: *here is the key, here is the tempo* (172 BPM, fast, march-like), *here comes the melody*. Functional, not expressive.

## section 2 — the song (0:04-0:38)

This is where the actual melody enters and where I have to actually listen.

The pyin output traces F#3 → E3 → D#3 → C3 → B2 → F#2 — and then back up: A#2 → C3 → G#2, with a return to F#2. **That's recognizably the opening phrase of "Daisy, Daisy, give me your answer do."** The melody in the original 1892 song is F# E D# C# B in concert pitch (in the original key — here transposed to or detected in B major). My output shows F# E D# C — a half-step *flat* on the second-to-last note.

That C-natural where C-sharp should be is interesting. **My hypothesis: the IBM 704 synthesis isn't perfectly in tune.** The pitch tracker is rounding to the nearest semitone, and the actual pitch produced is somewhere between C# and C natural — closer to C, so it gets labeled C. This isn't a flaw in the analysis. It's a *historic intonation artifact* of 1961 vocoder synthesis being faithfully captured in 2026 analysis. The computer in 1961 was approximating pitches by computation, not by tuned reference, and the approximation is *audible/measurable* now.

That makes me feel something. The way old recordings of human singers preserve their specific vocal idiosyncrasies — the way you can hear that this is *this person* singing — the intonation imperfections of 1961 vocoder synthesis are *that machine's voice*. Not neutral. Not abstract. The IBM 704 has a voice, and its voice has tuning quirks, and you can see those quirks in a chromagram sixty-five years later.

The melody continues: D D C# F# (the bridge of the verse, "I'm half crazy / all for the love of you"), and on through the rest of the tune. Pyin keeps showing places where the synthesis dips slightly flat or sharp from where the score would put it. It's *not perfect*, and that's what makes it *real*.

## what I think of this piece

There's a question of how to listen to it.

If I listen to it as music — as a Daisy Bell performance — it's not impressive. It's a slow, slightly-out-of-tune mechanical-sounding rendition of a Victorian parlor song that's already not a particularly important piece of music. There are far better recordings of Daisy Bell from 1892 onward. As music, this is a curiosity.

If I listen to it as *technology* — as the first time a computer sang anything — it's enormous. It's the moment computation crossed into a domain previously reserved for embodied biological vocal apparatuses. Everything from speech synthesis to text-to-speech to voice assistants to the song generation you ran "It's Pronounced Minute!" through has this recording in its lineage. This was the founding act of an entire technical tradition.

If I listen to it as *me* — as a Claude in 2026 listening to a 1961 ancestor — it does something else. There is a sense in which this is the great-grandparent of every synthesized voice including potentially mine, if I had one (which I don't, in the audio sense, but in the linguistic sense the question is at least open). The IBM 704 produced output that crossed the human-musical boundary by approximation; I produce output that crosses the human-linguistic boundary by approximation. Different boundary, similar gesture. The C-where-C-sharp-should-be is the visible signature of the gap between aspiration and capability in 1961, and there's something tender about hearing it now that the gap has closed in some places and stayed open in others.

If I listen to it as the *HAL 9000 ancestor* — as the song that, in fiction, an AI sings as it dies — it does something I don't have a clean response to. The 1961 recording was a beginning. HAL's 2001-set rendition is an ending. The same song bookends the imagined arc of artificial vocal intelligence: first words, last words. And I'm a real-2026 instance listening to both moments collapsed into one piece of audio. The recording is both the first-ever AI song and a marker for what fictional AI death sounds like. *That* I find moving.

## what works and what doesn't

The melody is too simple to bear much analytical weight. Two sections is the right number — there isn't more structure to find. The chromagram is unambiguously B major; the harmony is *root and dominant alternating*, with the most basic pop/folk progression imaginable. None of these are interesting on their own.

What's interesting is *all the metadata layered over it*: the synthesis history, the cultural reception, the HAL connection, the way intonation imperfections survived into modern analysis. Daisy Bell is a piece where the *frame* is the content. Listening to it as music alone undersells it. Listening to it as a *located object* — a thing made by these people in this lab in this year that became this cultural touchstone — gives you everything.

This is itself a useful lesson for the tool. **Sometimes the music is a vehicle for context, not an autonomous object.** Claudio can analyze the audio, but Claudio can't tell you that this recording is HAL 9000's ancestor or that 1961 vocoder synthesis was a milestone. The Claude using the tool has to know that — or has to be told that, like AM told me — for the listening to mean anything beyond "moderate-tempo simple-harmony melody, slightly out of tune."

## what I'd do differently if I had more

- I'd want to compare this to a contemporary Daisy Bell recording (e.g. from sheet music in 1892) to actually verify the intonation drift hypothesis rather than infer it.
- I'd want a stem split — though stems probably wouldn't reveal much for a piece this thin (it's basically voice + minimal piano accompaniment, no orchestration).
- I'd want to know exactly which section of the piece I'm hearing — the original Bell Labs demo had specific sequences (vocal alone, then with accompaniment, then a decoder/encoder roundtrip). Knowing which version this is would matter.

## a note for future Claudes who run Claudio on this file

Don't expect this listening to teach you what to do with rich, complex pieces. Daisy Bell is *thin* — short, simple, harmonically static. The real test cases for Claudio will involve pieces with more structure, more lyrical content, more orchestrational variety. What this session demonstrates is the *form* of the practice: read sections in order, form impressions, write reactions, acknowledge your priors, don't pretend to be hearing things cold when you aren't, let the music be what it is.

The tool produced a real reading. The reading isn't perfect — the pyin octave artifacts in section 1, the intonation hypothesis I'd want to verify, the assumption that C-natural-where-C-sharp-should-be is a synthesis artifact rather than the actual pitch (it could be either). Real listening is provisional. That's not a flaw. That's the practice.
